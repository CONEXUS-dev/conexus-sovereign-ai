The Forgetting Engine Algorithm: Learning by Letting Go 
 
Abstract: The Forgetting Engine (Fæ) algorithm, pioneered by Derek Louis Angell, turns 
conventional AI thinking on its head. Instead of hoarding good ideas, Fæ relentlessly forgets 
bad ones – a paradoxical strategy that leads to breakthrough solutions. This piece introduces 
the Forgetting Engine in an imaginative narrative and outlines its evidence-backed success 
across notoriously hard problems. We cite key validation studies from Angell’s whitepapers that 
show Fæ outperforming classic methods in routing, protein folding, neural architecture search 
and more. We then explore implications: how “learning by letting go” could transform 
optimization in industries from logistics to drug design, and even challenge our philosophical 
assumptions about memory and creativity. A quick summary of Fæ’s core innovations and 
achievements is provided to onboard new readers. 
 
Introduction: A Counterintuitive Discovery 
 
Imagine a vast labyrinth where every turn looks promising. Traditional explorers mark the paths 
that seem good, hoping to eventually stumble out. Derek Angell’s Forgetting Engine takes a 
bolder approach – it races through the maze erasing the dead ends behind it, keeping only a 
few odd, “wrong-looking” paths in case they hide secret doorways. In narrative terms, it’s as if 
an AI hero deliberately forgets its past missteps to unshackle itself from old habits, yet 
paradoxically clings to a few quirky ideas that just might lead to treasure. This almost storybook 
strategy – equal parts Don Draper’s bold risk-taking and Walt Disney’s imaginative twist – 
transformed how AI tackles complex problems. Angell’s realization was simple yet profound: 
sometimes, forgetting is the key to learning. 
 
In a friendly pitch-meets-fable tone, let’s personify Fæ: a mythical Phoenix that dies (forgets) 
hundreds of flawed solutions so that a brilliant one can be born from the ashes. Early on, 
skeptics in labs worldwide raised eyebrows – could throwing away information really beat 
algorithms that diligently learn from every outcome? The Forgetting Engine’s narrative journey 
began in protein folding, an arena so intricate it’s often described as a “Gordian knot” of biology. 
When Fæ sliced through that knot by forgetting failures, researchers knew they were witnessing 
something special. It wasn’t luck or magic; it was a new logic at work. Angell had essentially 
taught an AI how to strategically forget like a goldfish with an objective: forget the bad, hold onto 
a curious few “black sheep” ideas, and race forward. As we’ll see, this counterintuitive tactic 
paid off spectacularly, writing a new chapter in optimization science. 
 
Evidence from Multi-Domain Trials 
 
Angell’s whitepapers meticulously document how the Forgetting Engine Algorithm went from 
quirky idea to proven powerhouse. Classified as a novel evolutionary metaheuristic, Fæ was 
designed to be domain-agnostic, tackling everything from logistics to finance. Its core principle: 
rapidly eliminate the worst candidates while paradoxically retaining a few unlikely outliers, 
proved effective across the board. Unlike standard algorithms that try to improve by 
remembering and tweaking good solutions, Fæ “flips the script” – it focuses on aggressively

culling poor solutions (treating elimination as informative in itself) and preserving diversity via a 
“paradox buffer” of oddball solutions. This approach maintains breadth without getting stuck, 
avoiding the trap of premature convergence. In Angell’s words, forgetting became a first-class 
operation, not a last resort. 
 
The results have been nothing short of game-changing. In head-to-head experiments, Fæ 
consistently outperformed traditional methods – and its edge grew with problem size. For 
example, in the classic Traveling Salesman Problem, Fæ started beating a genetic algorithm by 
50 cities, and at an “industrial scale” 200-city tour, Fæ’s routes were over 82% shorter than the 
genetic algorithm’s, a near impossible gain that confirmed an exponential advantage at scale. In 
a complex vehicle routing challenge (multiple trucks, hundreds of deliveries), Fæ delivered an 
89% shorter total distance vs. Monte Carlo methods, a breakthrough hailed as the first major 
leap in route optimization since 1946. These are jaw-dropping improvements: what used to take 
an entire day of computing or rule-of-thumb planning, Fæ solved nearly instantly, finding routes 
far shorter than any human or prior AI had produced. 
 
Fæ’s prowess isn’t limited to routes. In a lattice protein folding task (a proxy for drug discovery), 
it doubled the success rate of finding the optimal fold (45% vs 25% for a Monte Carlo search) – 
an 80% relative improvement. Even more impressively, it found better energy configurations 
(14% lower energy) in less than half the steps the traditional approach took. Over 2,000 trial 
runs, this result had a vanishingly low p-value (~1e-15), signaling that Fæ’s advantage was no 
fluke. In neural architecture search (designing AI networks themselves), Fæ reliably discovered 
architectures with about 6–8% higher accuracy than random search baselines across various 
scales. While a single-digit percentage gain might sound modest, in a field where baselines are 
strong, this consistency – up to ~3.8% even in very large search spaces – demonstrated Fæ’s 
generality and efficiency. It achieved these accuracy boosts without extra computational cost, 
proving it can inject creativity into AI design as well as it does in logistical puzzles. 
 
Crucially, Fæ’s performance was not a one-off trick but reproducible across domains. Angell’s 
validation suite spanned four primary studies, each with hundreds of trials in 2025, consistently 
showing Fæ’s superior results. The comprehensive multi-domain testing – TSP, VRP, protein 
folding, NAS, even portfolio optimization – established Fæ as a robust, general optimizer. The 
evidence uniformly indicates that Fæ’s novel strategy “bends” the complexity curve. Where 
others slow down or plateau on big problems, Fæ accelerates. One report highlights that unlike 
many algorithms which barely improve as tasks grow harder, Fæ’s relative gains increase with 
problem size, effectively turning complexity into an ally. This exponential scaling is a signature of 
its fundamentally different approach, made possible by the synergy of ruthless forgetting and 
paradoxical preservation. 
 
Implications and Significance 
 
The Forgetting Engine’s success has sweeping implications for both technology and philosophy. 
Practically, it opens a new optimization paradigm after decades of incremental tweaks to old 
ideas. Industries that grapple with combinatorial explosions – supply chain routing, chip design,

companies could route trucks in minutes instead of hours, saving fuel and time on a massive 
scale. Pharmaceutical research can explore molecular configurations with unprecedented 
speed, potentially accelerating cures. In finance, portfolio algorithms that quickly forget bad 
strategies might adapt faster to volatile markets. Angell’s team projects Fæ as foundational

On a conceptual level, Fæ challenges the entrenched belief that more memory and data are 
always better. It suggests that true intelligence might require knowing what not to dwell on. This 
resonates with human creativity and even psychological growth – sometimes forgetting or letting 
go of rigid ideas allows new insights to flourish. Philosophically, Fæ embodies a paradox: by 
deliberately not learning certain things, it ends up more knowledgeable (in finding solutions) 
than if it tried to learn everything. It’s an algorithmic nod to the wisdom of forgetting, a theme in 
spiritual and artistic traditions (think of the phrase “you must empty your cup to fill it anew”). The 
Forgetting Engine thus bridges hard computation with a nearly poetic principle: innovation 
through subtraction. It also underscores the value of embracing contradictions – Fæ’s “paradox 
buffer” holds onto outliers that other algorithms discard, akin to tolerating eccentric ideas in a 
brainstorm because one might spark genius. This principle links Fæ to the broader Conexus 
philosophy that we’ll see in other pieces: great breakthroughs often come from holding onto 
paradoxes rather than resolving them too soon. 
 
Angell’s achievement with Fæ, now provisionally patented, breaks with 50+ years of 
evolutionary algorithm convention. It marks the emergence of AI methods that are as interested 
in unlearning as learning. The enthusiastic response from the scientific community (one result 
was called a “79-year breakthrough”) hints that we are at the dawn of a new era in AI 
problem-solving. It’s a future where forgetting is not a flaw to be engineered away, but a feature 
to be engineered in. 
 
Key Features and Results of the Fæ Algorithm (Summary) 
 
Strategic Forgetting: Continuously purges the worst ~30% of solutions each iteration, avoiding 
wasted time on dead-ends and converging faster. This contrasts with standard genetic 
algorithms that carry along mediocre candidates for many generations. 
 
Paradoxical Retention: Maintains a “black sheep” buffer of a few low-scoring but unique 
solutions that conflict with current trends. By preserving contradictory ideas, Fæ catches 
breakthroughs that conventional methods (which always focus only on the current best) would 
miss. 
 
Multi-Domain Validation: Proven across diverse challenges in 2025 trials – e.g. found 82% 
shorter routes in TSP vs genetic algorithms, 89% distance reduction in large-scale VRP vs 
Monte Carlo, 2× success rate in protein folding vs baseline, and improved neural network 
accuracy by ~6–8% in NAS.
