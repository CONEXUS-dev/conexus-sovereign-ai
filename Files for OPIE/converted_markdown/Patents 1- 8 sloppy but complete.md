 Abstract 
 A system and method for facilitating collaborative art creation through a multi 
modal, iterative human-AI interaction process, herein termed the "Generative Cadence 
 Canvas." The invention enables users to sequentially contribute to evolving artworks across 
 various modalities (e.g., visual, textual, audio, video) within defined time limits. The 
 system leverages generative AI models to interpret human inputs, offer diverse creative 
 transformations, and manage the sequential flow with controlled visibility, thereby fostering 
 unique and unpredictable artistic outcomes. All resulting collaborative artworks are 
 dedicated to the public domain, emphasizing the process of co-creation over individual 
 ownership. 
 Field of the Invention 
 The present invention relates generally to artificial intelligence (AI) systems, human 
computer interaction, digital art creation, collaborative software platforms, and generative 
 media. More specifically, it pertains to novel methods for orchestrating human and AI 
 collaboration in a sequential, multi-modal creative process. 
 Background of the Invention 
 The digital creative landscape has seen significant advancements in both 
 collaborative tools and generative AI: 
 Traditional Collaborative Art Platforms: Existing platforms facilitate human-to 
human collaboration, often through shared digital canvases (e.g., Drawpile, Miro Lite) or 
 sequential "telephone game" / "exquisite corpse" formats (e.g., Skwiz, MoMA Exquisite 
Corpse, Tel-AI-phone). While engaging, these often lack integrated, intelligent AI 
 responsiveness across multiple modalities. 
 Generative AI Tools: Modern generative AI models can produce high-quality 
 content from text prompts across images (e.g., DALL-E 3, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, 
 Adobe Firefly), music (e.g., Suno AI, Udio, Mubert, Google Lyria), and video (e.g., Sora, 
 Runway, Synthesia). 
 Human-AI Co-creation: Research indicates that effective human-AI co-creation 
 involves a "dance" where AI augments human creativity, helping overcome blocks and 
 explore ideas, with humans providing "judgment" and "taste". However, a significant 
 limitation is AI's tendency to produce "homogenous" or "generic" outputs when left to its 
 own devices, lacking true originality or nuanced context. 
 Limitations in Current Integration: A discernible gap exists in platforms that can 
 truly facilitate a fluid, real-time, multimodal interaction within existing digital co-creation 
 environments. Current AI tools often function as "black boxes" lacking granular human 
 control or explicit collaborative features. Furthermore, the legal and ethical frameworks for 
 AI-generated content, particularly concerning authorship and ownership, remain 
 ambiguous, with purely AI-generated works generally not being copyrightable. 
 Summary of the Invention 
 The present invention provides a novel system and method for multi-modal, iterative 
 human-AI collaborative art creation, referred to as the "Generative Cadence Canvas." The 
 invention addresses the limitations of existing creative tools by providing an intuitive

 platform where users engage in a structured, sequential "telephone game" of art, with 
 generative AI acting as a transformative co-creator at each step. The core of the invention 
 lies in its unique process: 
 1. Sequential, Time-Boxed Contributions: Users contribute in defined turns, each 
 with a strict time limit (e.g., 30 seconds), fostering rapid, intuitive creativity. 
 2. Controlled Visibility: Projects are initially obscured (e.g., blurred) to maintain an 
 element of surprise, revealing full content only upon a user's commitment to 
 contribute. 
 3. Multimodal AI Orchestration: At each turn, a human provides input (text, 
 sketch, audio, or video clip), and generative AI (e.g., Google Gemini via Vertex AI 
 Live API) processes this input to generate multiple, diverse multimodal outputs. 
 4. Human-in-the-Loop Curation & Refinement: The human user selects their 
 preferred AI-generated output and can further refine it (e.g., drawing overlays, text 
 edits, masking) before passing it to the next participant. This ensures human 
 judgment and "taste" guide the creative evolution, counteracting AI's tendency 
 towards homogeneity. 
5. Public Domain Output: All resulting collaborative artworks are explicitly 
 dedicated to the public domain, simplifying intellectual property ambiguities and 
 emphasizing the shared joy of creation over individual ownership. 
 6. Showroom & Community Features: Completed "Canvas Chains" are showcased 
 in a gallery, with clear attribution to all human and AI contributors, fostering a 
 positive and engaged community. 
 The "Generative Cadence Canvas" framework ensures a dynamic "dance" between 
 human and AI, leading to genuinely unique, unpredictable, and collaborative artistic 
 expressions at scale.

 Method of Operation 
 The "Generative Cadence Canvas" operates as follows: 
 1. User Onboarding & Choice: A user signs up/logs in. They are presented with two 
 primary options: "Start New Creation" or "Continue Someone Else's." If "Continue 
 Someone Else's" is selected, a list of ongoing projects is displayed. These projects are 
 initially blurred or abstractly represented to maintain an element of surprise, revealing full 
 content only upon selection. 
 2. Project Initiation: If "Start New Creation" is chosen, the user is presented with a 
 blank canvas. The user provides the initial creative input, which can be: a text prompt (e.g., 
 "A serene forest with glowing mushrooms"), a simple sketch or image upload, or a short 
 audio recording (e.g., a sound effect or melody). The initiator also defines game parameters, 
 such as the number of turns (e.g., 4-6) and privacy (public or private). 
 3. AI Generation & Human Refinement Loop: 
 Turn Allocation: Only one user can contribute to a project at a time. If multiple 
 users attempt to join, they are placed in a queue with an estimated wait time. 
 Time Limit: Once a user's turn begins, a strict 30-second timer (or a creator 
defined 2-minute limit) starts, encouraging rapid, intuitive creativity. 
 AI Interpretation & Generation: The AI processes the previous contribution's 
 output (or the initial prompt) and the current user's input (if any). It then generates 
 multiple, diverse multimodal options (e.g., 3-4 images based on text, 3-4 text 
 descriptions based on an image, 3-4 audio clips based on text). 
 Human Selection & Refinement: The user reviews the AI-generated options and 
 selects their preferred output. They can then use basic refinement tools (e.g., 
 drawing overlays, text edits, masking) or provide further text prompts for targeted 
 AI regeneration, ensuring human control and artistic direction. Once 
 selected/refined, the user "commits" their contribution. 
 4. Sequential Chain Continuation: The system ensures that the modality of 
 input/output changes or evolves with each turn to encourage diverse creative interpretations 
 (e.g., text to image, image to audio, audio to text). Steps 3 and 4 repeat with subsequent 
 users until the predefined number of turns is completed. 
 5. Final Reveal & Showroom: Once all turns are complete, the entire "Canvas 
 Chain" is revealed to all participants, showcasing the progression of the artwork from the 
initial prompt through each human and AI transformation. Completed projects are displayed 
 in a public showroom/gallery. Clear attribution is provided for all human and AI 
 contributors within the final display. Users can download the final composite artwork and 
 share it directly to social media platforms.

 Method and System for Generating Copyrightable Works via User-Authored, Fine-Tuned

 Inventor: Derek Angell, Orlando, FL 
 Background of the Invention 
 The present invention addresses the "Human Authorship Gap" in AI-generated 
 works. Under current copyright law in the United States, a creative work must include 
 meaningful human input to be eligible for protection. Simply typing a prompt into an AI 
 does not satisfy this requirement. As a result, creators working with generative AI often find 


---

## Page 8

 themselves unable to claim ownership of their own output, despite meaningful contributions 
 in style, intent, and selection. This gap poses a significant legal and creative barrier in the 
 AI era. 
 The present invention offers a novel solution: a system that allows users to become 
 the authors of their own fine-tuned AI models, transforming them from prompt writers into 
 toolmakers—and thereby establishing a legal foundation for copyrightable output. 
 Summary of the Invention 
 This invention introduces a method for creating Mirror Models—personalized 
 generative AI models fine-tuned from a curated user dataset (e.g., art, photography, or 
 sketches). The system includes: 
 1. A user-curated dataset upload interface 
2. Metadata and trigger word assignment tools 
 3. An integrated fine-tuning backend using LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) 
 4. Cryptographic linking between model and user account 
 5. A provenance logging engine that captures the full authorship trail 
 When the user generates content with their Mirror Model, they are not simply using 
 an off-the-shelf AI—they are activating a tool they authored through creative selection, 
 direction, and design. This paradigm shift enables strong copyright claims over the 
 generated work. 
 Detailed Description of the Invention 
 Phase 1: Model Authoring (Creating the 'Mirror Image') 
 1. User-Curated Dataset Submission 
 The user uploads 10–30 original images that reflect their unique style or subject matter. The 
 system prompts the user to affirm legal ownership and authorship of the dataset. 
 2. Captioning and Trigger Word Assignment 
 Each image is captioned by the user or reviewed/edited based on AI suggestions. The user 
 selects a unique trigger word (e.g., my-echoform-style) that activates their model. 
 3. LoRA Fine-Tuning Process 
 The system applies Low-Rank Adaptation to a large base model (e.g., Stable Diffusion XL), 
 generating a lightweight, efficient model file (10–200 MB). The resulting Mirror Model is 
 cryptographically linked to the user's account. 
 Phase 2: Authored Generation + Legal Provenance 
 1. Invoking the Authored Model 
 During image creation within CONEXUS, the user selects their custom model. 
 2. Generating the Artwork 
 The user includes their trigger word in the prompt to invoke the fine-tuned style. The 
 system applies their authored model to generate the image. 
 3. Provenance Report Logging 
 Each generation is accompanied by a Provenance Report, enhanced with: 
 Model ID and cryptographic hash 
 Timestamp of generation 
 Dataset manifest and caption archive 
 User authorship declaration 
This process ensures a verifiable, continuous chain of authorship from the curated

 Thus, the user becomes the legal author of the tool (the LoRA model), and by

 System Overview and Core Metaphor 
 The present invention relates to a computer-implemented system and method for 
 generating a verifiable record of human authorship in creative works produced with the 
 assistance of artificial intelligence (AI). The invention addresses a significant and emergent 
 technical problem rooted in modern intellectual property law: the difficulty of establishing 
 and evidencing the quantum of human creative contribution required for copyright 
 protection in an era of increasingly sophisticated generative AI tools. 
 Recent legal precedent, most notably the U.S. Court of Appeals decision in Thaler v. 
 Perlmutter, has affirmed the U.S. Copyright Office's (USCO) long-standing human 
 authorship requirement, ruling that a work must be authored by a human being to be eligible 
 for copyright. This principle has been consistently applied by the USCO, which denies 
 copyright to works generated purely by AI and requires a showing of "meaningful human 
 input" or "sufficient human control" for works created with AI assistance. 
 To render this complex process of authorship documentation intuitive and accessible 
 to creators who may not be legal experts, the system's user interface (UI) is architected 
around a guiding interface metaphor. Two primary embodiments of this metaphor are 
 contemplated: 
 1. The "Authorship Forge" Metaphor: In this embodiment, the user is cast as an 
 artisan, a "smith," who is not merely prompting a machine but is actively "forging" a 
 creative work. The UI employs visuals and terminology associated with a smithy. Raw AI 
generated content is the "base metal." The user's actions are framed as "hammering" 
 (iterative editing), "shaping" (direct manipulation of the output), "quenching" (selecting the 
 best from multiple variations), and "polishing" (final refinements). 
 2. The "Creative Trial" Metaphor: This embodiment frames the creative process 
 as a legal proceeding where the user is a "litigant" building a "case" for their authorship. 
 Each creative choice—every prompt refinement, image selection, or text edit—is treated as 
 an "exhibit" of evidence. 
 The Human-AI Collaboration Module 
 The Human-AI Collaboration Module forms the core interactive environment of the 
 system. It consists of a sophisticated user interface and a robust backend architecture 
 designed to manage the dialogue between the human user and one or more integrated 
 generative AI models, which may include text-to-image generators, large language models 
 (LLMs), or other creative AI systems. 
 The module is specifically engineered to capture evidence of the following legally 
 significant creative activities: 
 Iterative Refinement: The system encourages and logs a multi-step, conversational 
 process. A user may begin with a simple prompt, receive an initial output, and then engage 
 in a series of refinements, such as modifying the prompt, adjusting control parameters (e.g., 
 style strength, chaos, seed values), or providing feedback. 
 Creative Selection and Arrangement: This feature directly addresses the precedent 
 set in the copyright case of Zarya of the Dawn, where the USCO found Kristina 
 Kashtanova's selection, coordination, and arrangement of AI-generated images to be a 
 copyrightable contribution, even while denying copyright to the individual images 
 themselves.

 Direct Modification and Elaboration: The system provides integrated tools for the

 The Gamification Engine 
 The Gamification Engine is a novel component that applies established principles of 
 game design to the unique technical problem of structuring and incentivizing the creation of 
a legally significant authorship record. The primary purpose of the engine is not merely to 
 make the creative process "fun"—an activity many users already find intrinsically 
 rewarding. Instead, its core function is to guide, motivate, and reward the user for 
 performing the specific, discrete actions that build a robust and defensible case for human 
 authorship under the legal standards established by the USCO and courts. 
 The engine is architected to leverage powerful psychological drivers to shape user 
 behavior, drawing on principles of behavioral psychology and established gamification 
 frameworks: 
 Competence and Mastery: Users are motivated by the desire to improve their skills 
 and feel a sense of competence. The engine facilitates this by awarding "Experience Points" 
 (XP) for each creative action performed. More complex or legally significant actions award 
 more XP. 
 Achievement and Accomplishment: The engine provides a system of virtual 
 rewards in the form of badges and achievements that recognize specific creative milestones. 
 These are directly mapped to actions that demonstrate authorship. 
 Social Influence and Community: The system includes optional social features, 
 such as leaderboards that rank users based on their "Creativity Contribution Score" and a 
 gallery where users can showcase their works along with their authorship credentials. 
 The Algorithmic Creativity Assessment (ACA) Module 
 A core inventive aspect of the system is the Algorithmic Creativity Assessment 
 (ACA) module. This module introduces a novel, computer-implemented method for 
 algorithmically analyzing and quantifying the user's creative contribution in real-time. The 
 ACA module adapts established dimensions from psychological tests of divergent thinking, 
 such as the Alternative Uses Test (AUT), to this novel technological context. The key 
 metrics are: 
 1. Fluency: A quantitative measure of the user's overall activity. It is calculated based 
 on the total number of interactions, the number of distinct creative iterations, and the 
 volume of AI variants generated and considered by the user. 
 2. Flexibility: A measure of the conceptual breadth of the user's exploration. It is 
 calculated by analyzing the semantic and/or visual diversity of the generated outputs. 
 3. Originality: A measure of the novelty of the final work. It is calculated by 
 measuring the semantic or visual distance between the user's final selected and edited 
 output and baseline outputs for similar prompts. 
 4. Elaboration: A direct measure of the user's transformative work. It is quantified 
 by the number and significance of direct edits, modifications, and additions made by the 
 user to the AI's output. 
These individual metrics are then combined by the ACA module into a proprietary,

 weighted, composite score designated as the "Creativity Contribution Score" (CCS). 
 The Cryptographic Provenance Ledger 
 To ensure the integrity and legal admissibility of the data generated by the system, 
 the invention incorporates a Cryptographic Provenance Ledger module. This module 
 implements a system for creating an immutable, tamper-evident audit trail of the entire 
 creative process, conceptually similar to the Coalition for Content Provenance and 
 Authenticity (C2PA) standard but applied in a novel, highly granular manner. 
 Using the user's unique cryptographic credentials, the system generates a digital 
 signature and a secure timestamp for every legally significant action, effectively creating a 
 chain of evidence. The ultimate output of this module is not just the final creative work but 
 also a comprehensive, self-contained Provenance Report.

BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION  
The Problem with Traditional Optimization  
Traditional optimization algorithms, including Monte Carlo methods developed in the 1940s,  
have dominated computational optimization for nearly eight decades. While foundational, these  
methods suffer from critical limitations:  
• Inefficient exploration of high-dimensional solution spaces  
• Poor convergence rates in deceptive optimization landscapes  
• Frequent entrapment in local optima  
• Computational waste on clearly non-viable solution paths  
• Inability to leverage contradictory information for breakthrough discoveries  
The Need for Paradigm Shift  
Modern challenges in protein folding, neural architecture search, financial modeling, and other  
domains demand optimization methods that achieve superior performance with limited  
computational resources. A fundamentally new approach is required.  
SUMMARY OF THE INVENTION  
The Forgetting Engine: A Revolutionary Approach  
The invention introduces the Forgetting Engine, which reconceptualizes optimization as an  
elimination-based rather than search-based process. Instead of seeking optimal solutions  
through exploration, the method strategically eliminates non-viable candidates while preserving  
paradoxical or contradictory candidates that conventional methods would discard.

• Strategic Elimination as a Computational Primitive: Aggressive pruning of  
worst-performing candidates each iteration.  
• Paradox Retention Mechanism: Identifies and preserves contradictory candidates with  
long-term promise.
