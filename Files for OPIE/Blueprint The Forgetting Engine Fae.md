Blueprint: The Forgetting Engine (Fae) 
This document outlines the philosophy, components, and process of the Fae algorithm, a 
revolutionary approach to solving complex problems. 
Part 1: The Core Idea - Creation Through Negation 
The Fae is based on a simple but profound shift in thinking. 
• 
Traditional algorithms try to find the one "right" answer by searching through a 
massive space of possibilities. This is like trying to find a needle in a haystack. It's slow 
and often gets stuck. 
• 
The Forgetting Engine (Fae) finds the right answer by intelligently and aggressively 
eliminating all the "wrong" answers. 
Think of it like a sculptor. A sculptor doesn't "build" a statue; they find it by removing all the 
pieces of stone that are not the statue. Or, to use our own aphorism: "The map completes itself 
by erasing the roads." Fae solves problems by simplifying the universe of possibilities until the 
correct answer is all that remains. 
Part 2: The Engine's Components 
The engine consists of a few key modules that work together: 
1. The Population Manager: Manages a "crowd" of many different potential solutions all 
at once. 
2. The Evaluation Framework: Looks at every solution in the crowd and scores how 
"good" it is. 
3. The Strategic Elimination Engine: This is the heart of Fae. It takes the scores and 
deletes, or "forgets," a large percentage of the worst-performing solutions. 
4. The Paradox Detection Module: A special, highly intelligent filter that spots "weird" 
solutions that look bad but might hold the key to a breakthrough. It saves these from 
being "forgotten." 
Part 3: The Fae Algorithm - Step-by-Step 
The engine runs in a continuous loop. Here’s how one cycle works: 
1. Start with a Crowd (Initialization): The process begins by creating a large population 
(e.g., 50) of random, potential solutions to the problem. 
2. Score Everyone (Evaluation): The engine evaluates every single solution in the crowd 
and ranks them from best to worst based on a scoring system. 
3. The Forgetting (Strategic Elimination): The engine permanently deletes the worst-
performing solutions (e.g., the bottom 30%). This is the aggressive "forgetting" step that 
constantly pushes the population toward better solutions.

4. Save the "Weirdos" (Paradox Retention): Before deleting the worst solutions, the 
Paradox Detection module scans them. It looks for solutions that, despite their bad score, 
have unique or promising structural features. It "saves" these few from being forgotten, 
preserving diversity and preventing the engine from getting stuck. 
5. Refill the Crowd (Replenishment): The engine uses the surviving "good" and 
"paradoxical" solutions to create new ones (through mutation and recombination) until 
the crowd is back to its original size. 
6. Repeat: The engine repeats this cycle of evaluating, forgetting, saving, and replenishing. 
With each loop, the overall quality of the crowd gets better and better, rapidly converging 
on a final, optimal solution. 
Part 4: A Simple Example - The 4-City Problem 
Imagine trying to find the shortest route between 4 cities arranged in a rectangle. 
• 
A traditional algorithm might start at one city and just travel to the next closest one, a 
"greedy" approach that can easily lead to a bad final route. 
• 
Fae starts differently. It first looks at all possible connections, including the long 
diagonal ones. It identifies these long diagonals as being part of the "worst solutions" and 
forgets them immediately. With the bad routes erased from the map, the only path left is 
the simple, optimal one around the perimeter.
