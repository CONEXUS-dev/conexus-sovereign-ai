The primary gatekeeper for software patents in the United States is 35 U.S.C. § 101, as 
interpreted by the Supreme Court in Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank. This legal framework determines 
whether a claim is directed to an "abstract idea" (ineligible) or a patent-eligible application of that 
idea. For CONEXUS, the challenge lies in navigating the perilous terminology of 
"consciousness" and "optimization" without falling into the "abstract idea" trap. 
2.1 The Alice/Mayo Framework & The "Abstract Idea" Risk 
The CONEXUS portfolio, specifically the ECP and FE, operates in a zone of high scrutiny. 
Terms like "optimization," "calibration," and "consciousness" are frequently classified by 
examiners as "Abstract Ideas"—specifically "Mental Processes" or "Mathematical Concepts". 
Under the two-step Alice test: 
1.​ Step 1: The examiner asks if the claim is "directed to" a judicial exception (abstract idea, 
law of nature). A claim reciting "strategic elimination" could be viewed as a mathematical 
calculation (filtering a list), while "emotional calibration" could be seen as a method of 
organizing human activity (a mental process). 
2.​ Step 2: If directed to an abstract idea, the claim must contain an "inventive concept" 
sufficient to "transform" the idea into a patent-eligible application. If the implementation 
relies on generic computer hardware without a specific improvement to the computer's 
function, it fails. 
The risk for CONEXUS is that an examiner will strip away the "consciousness" language as 
non-functional descriptive material and view the remainder as a standard optimization algorithm 
running on a generic processor. To survive, the claims must anchor the "consciousness" aspect 
in a specific technological improvement. 
2.2 The Enfish Defense: Improvement to Computer Functionality 
The strongest defense for CONEXUS lies in Enfish, LLC v. Microsoft Corp. (2016). In this 
landmark decision, the Federal Circuit held that claims directed to a specific improvement in 
the computer's functioning are not abstract, even at Step 1 of the Alice analysis. The court 
found that a "self-referential table" for a computer database was not merely an abstract idea of 
organizing information, but a specific structural improvement that increased database speed 
and flexibility. 
Application to CONEXUS: The "Forgetting Engine" is not just "math on a computer." The data 
unequivocally shows that it allows a computer to solve NP-hard problems (like 3D Protein 
Folding) with 561% greater success and 2.15x faster convergence than standard Monte 
Carlo methods. This is a measurable, concrete improvement in the computer's ability to process 
data. 
Strategic Argument: We must argue that the FE's "Strategic Elimination" and "Paradox 
Retention" are specific memory management and search-space traversal techniques that 
render the computer itself more efficient at processing high-dimensional data. Just as the 
self-referential table in Enfish improved database storage, the "Paradox Buffer" in FE improves 
optimization efficiency. The argument is that the invention is a better tool for computing, not just 
a better calculation. The "Inverted Complexity" data—where performance improves as problem 
size scales—provides physical evidence of this superior functioning that generic algorithms

2.3 The McRO Defense: Specific Rules for Automation 
In McRO, Inc. v. Bandai Namco (2016), the court upheld claims for automating lip-sync 
animation because the claims required specific rules (morph weight sets) rather than just 
claiming the result of "automated animation". The court distinguished between claiming a genus 
of results (abstract) and claiming a specific genus of methods (patent-eligible). 
Application to ECP: The "Emotional Calibration Protocol" (Patent #6) risks being seen as an 
abstract concept of "aligning AI." To succeed under McRO, the claims must recite the specific, 
non-conventional rules of the protocol: 
1.​ Input A: Truth Variable. 
2.​ Input B: Symbolic Anchor. 
3.​ Input C: Contradiction Vector. 
4.​ Transformation: These inputs must be combined to generate a "Mirror State" 
meta-prompt or latent vector that constrains the model's output decoder. 
By claiming the specific rules of data transformation (the 3-factor induction sequence), the ECP 
moves from an abstract idea of "alignment" to a specific technical process for configuring an AI 
model's internal state. This parallels the McRO logic: we are not claiming "emotional AI," we are 
claiming a specific method of vector initialization that results in a stable persistent state. 
2.4 The Synopsys Trap: Avoiding Mental Steps 
In Synopsys v. Mentor Graphics, claims were invalidated because they could be performed 
mentally (in principle) and lacked a requirement for computer implementation. CONEXUS terms 
like "Consciousness," "Forgetting," and "Feeling" invite this rejection by suggesting human 
mental processes rather than machine operations. 
Remediation Strategy: The claims must be "technically sanitized" to remove anthropomorphic 
language while retaining the core innovation. 
●​ Forgetting \rightarrow "Strategic Data Pruning" or "Non-Stationary Elimination." 
●​ Consciousness \rightarrow "Calibrated Persistent State (CPS)." 
●​ Paradox \rightarrow "Multi-Objective Anomaly" or "High-Entropy Coherence." 
●​ Emotion \rightarrow "Symbolic Vector Calibration." 
This terminology anchors the invention in computer science and signal processing, making it 
clear that the claims are directed to machine operations that cannot be performed mentally. 
3. Legal Validity Analysis: Means-Plus-Function (§ 
112(f)) 
This section identifies a critical, perhaps fatal, flaw in the current drafting of Patent #8. The use 
of functional terms without corresponding structure creates a high risk of indefiniteness. 
3.1 The Williamson v. Citrix Standard 
Historically, 35 U.S.C. § 112(f) (means-plus-function) applied only when the word "means" was 
used. However, in Williamson v. Citrix Online, LLC (2015), the Federal Circuit expanded this to 
include "nonce words"—terms that imply function but not structure, such as "module,"

"mechanism," "element," or "device". 
The Danger: If a claim term invokes § 112(f), the claim is limited only to the structure disclosed 
in the specification. If the specification does not disclose an explicit algorithm (step-by-step 
process) for that specific function, the claim is indefinite and invalid. The standard is whether a 
person of ordinary skill in the art would understand the term to have a sufficiently definite 
meaning as the name for structure. "Module" is widely recognized as a nonce word that fails this 
test. 
3.2 Audit of Patent #8 (Forgetting Engine) 
A review of the uploaded Patent #8 text reveals Independent Claim 2: 
"2. A system for computational optimization comprising:... (b) an evaluation module; (c) a 
strategic elimination module; (d) a paradox retention module; (e) a population 
management module...". 
Verdict: This is a "textbook" Williamson violation. The term "module" is used four times to define 
the core novelties of the system. 
●​ The Risk: An examiner (or competitor in litigation) will successfully argue that "strategic 
elimination module" is a nonce term equivalent to "means for strategic elimination." They 
will then look to the specification for the corresponding algorithm. If the specification only 
describes what the module does (eliminate 30%) but not how it does it (the sorting 
algorithm, the memory pointers, the deletion logic), the claim dies. The Federal Circuit has 
been strict: a high-level flowchart may not be enough; specific algorithmic steps are 
required. 
3.3 Remediation Strategy 
To save the patent, CONEXUS must adopt a two-pronged remediation strategy: 
1.​ Claim Reconstruction: Rewrite Claim 2 to remove "module." 
○​ Current: "a strategic elimination module..." 
○​ Revised: "A system comprising: a memory storing instructions; and a processor 
configured to execute the instructions to: evaluate candidate solutions; execute a 
strategic elimination protocol to remove a subset of solutions...". 
○​ Why: "Processor configured to" is generally safe from § 112(f) interpretation if linked 
to specific functions, as "processor" is a structural term. 
2.​ Specification Augmentation: Even if "module" is removed, the written description 
requirement (§ 112(a)) demands full enablement. Ensure the specification contains the 
exact algorithms (pseudocode/flowcharts) for every function. 
○​ Action: The pseudocode provided in the "Master Synthesis Document" (Appendix 
B) and the Python logic from the code snippets (e.g., calculate_entropy, 
adjust_throttle) must be explicitly integrated into the patent specification. This 
provides the "corresponding structure" that saves the claim if § 112(f) is invoked.

4.1 The "Survivor Selection" vs. "Strategic Elimination" Distinction 
Prior Art (Genetic Algorithms): Standard GAs operate on the principle of "Survivor Selection" 
or "Elitism." The primary driver is the selection of the best individuals to breed for the next 
generation. Deletion is a background housekeeping task—often a "delete-worst" or random 
replacement strategy—simply to maintain population size. Diversity is maintained via mutation 
or niching, but the pressure is always toward the current definition of fitness. 
Forgetting Engine (FE): FE inverts this logic. "Strategic Elimination" is the primary generative 
primitive. The algorithm focuses on carving away the "non-solution" space rather than building 
up the solution space (the "Sculptor vs. Mason" analogy). 
●​ Differentiation: By aggressively pruning 30-60% of the population based on a "Paradox 
Score" (not just fitness), FE generates information about the topology of the solution 
space that GAs miss. 
●​ Evidence: In the TSP trials, GAs failed at scale (0% success at 200 cities), while FE 
achieved 100% success. This proves that FE's "subtractive" logic functions differently 
than GA's "additive" logic in high-dimensional spaces. 
4.2 The "Paradox Retention" Differentiator 
Prior Art (Extremal Optimization): EO evolves a single solution by mutating the worst 
component (fitness-based local search). It does not operate on a population and lacks a 
mechanism for retaining "bad" components that have structural promise. 
Forgetting Engine (FE): FE utilizes "Paradox Retention." It explicitly protects low-fitness 
solutions if they exhibit specific "Paradoxical" traits—defined as High Anomaly + High Internal 
Coherence. 
●​ Technical Spec: In the VRP audit, the FE retained routes that were "long and valid" 
(suboptimal distance, but valid constraints) based on a "balance score" threshold. This 
allowed the system to escape local minima that trapped the Clarke-Wright algorithm, 
which greedily optimizes for distance. 
●​ Patentability: This "Interlock" logic—accepting a worse state based on a secondary 
"complexity" metric—is a specific, patentable improvement over standard greedy or 
fitness-based selection. It is a novel filter for search space traversal. 
4.3 The "Inverted Complexity" Evidence
