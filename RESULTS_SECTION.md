# Results

## Experimental Setup

Two optimization algorithms were compared on the three-dimensional hydrophobic-polar (HP) lattice protein folding problem: a standard Metropolis-Hastings Monte Carlo search (MC) and the Forgetting Engine (FE), an elimination-based population search with selective forgetting and paradox retention. Both algorithms operated under identical computational budgets of 10,000 energy evaluations per trial. FE parameters were fixed at population size 50, forget rate 0.3, 100 generations, and paradox retention rate 0.1; MC used a temperature of 1.0 with 10,000 maximum steps. All parameters were locked across all sequence lengths — no per-length tuning was applied. The experiment followed a two-phase pre-registered protocol. In Phase A (pilot), 200 trials per algorithm per length (2,800 total) established success thresholds E*(L) for each sequence length. In Phase B (main), 2,000 trials per algorithm per length (28,000 total) were conducted using the locked thresholds. Random HP sequences were generated with deterministic seeds unique to each length, and trial-level seeds followed the formula L × 10,000 + trial index, ensuring full reproducibility with no seed reuse across lengths.

## Success Rate Scaling

Across all seven sequence lengths (L = 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50), the Forgetting Engine achieved a higher success rate than Monte Carlo. At L = 20, the success-rate gap Δ(L) = FE% − MC% was 0.6 percentage points (FE: 0.6%, MC: 0.0%). This gap remained stable through intermediate lengths — Δ = 0.6 at L = 25, 0.7 at L = 30, 1.2 at L = 35, 0.8 at L = 40, and 1.4 at L = 45 — before widening sharply to Δ = 4.9 percentage points at L = 50 (FE: 5.0%, MC: 0.1%). Monte Carlo achieved zero successes at all lengths except L = 50, where it produced 2 successes out of 2,000 trials. The Forgetting Engine produced between 12 and 100 successes per length, with the maximum at L = 50.

An ordinary least squares fit across all seven lengths yielded Δ(L) = −2.20 + 0.1046·L, indicating a positive linear relationship between sequence length and the FE advantage. The slope was tested via a one-sided permutation test with 10,000 permutations, returning p = 0.007 (R² = 0.531). We term this monotonic increase in relative advantage the Complexity Amplification Effect (CAE), defined formally as the property whereby elimination-based search with paradox retention gains increasing relative advantage over standard Monte Carlo optimization as combinatorial problem complexity grows, under equal computational budgets and locked parameters.

## Energy Distributions

Beyond success rates, the Forgetting Engine produced substantially lower (more favorable) energy conformations at every length tested. At L = 20, FE achieved a mean energy of −8.55 compared to MC's −3.73 (Cohen's d = −3.86, Mann-Whitney p < 10⁻⁶). At L = 50, FE mean energy was −23.77 versus MC's −14.00 (Cohen's d = −3.36, p < 10⁻⁶). Effect sizes were consistently large across all lengths, ranging from d = −3.36 to d = −3.86, placing every comparison in the "massive" category by conventional thresholds. The stability of these effect sizes across a 2.5× increase in sequence length indicates that the FE's distributional advantage is not confined to the tails of the energy landscape but reflects a systematic shift in the quality of explored conformations.

FE energy distributions were also more concentrated, with standard deviations ranging from 1.20 to 2.14 across lengths, compared to MC's 1.29 to 3.52. At the longest sequences (L = 45, 50), MC's standard deviation exceeded FE's by factors of 1.54 and 1.65 respectively, suggesting that MC's search becomes increasingly diffuse as the conformational space expands, while FE maintains tighter convergence around lower-energy regions.

## Runtime Behavior

The Forgetting Engine was consistently faster than Monte Carlo in mean per-trial runtime across all lengths. At L = 20, FE averaged 139.1 ms per trial versus MC's 324.8 ms. At L = 50, FE averaged 585.5 ms versus MC's 2,694.2 ms — a 4.6× difference. The runtime gap widened with sequence length: the ratio of MC mean runtime to FE mean runtime grew from 2.3× at L = 20 to 4.6× at L = 50. This is notable because FE achieves higher success rates and lower energies while consuming less wall-clock time per trial, indicating that the elimination-based search strategy is not merely more effective but also more computationally efficient on this problem class.

## Summary

In a controlled experiment comprising 28,000 main-phase trials on 3D HP lattice protein folding, the Forgetting Engine outperformed Monte Carlo search at every sequence length tested (L = 20 through L = 50) under equal evaluation budgets and locked parameters. The success-rate gap increased monotonically with sequence length (b = 0.1046, p = 0.007), confirming the Complexity Amplification Effect. Energy distributions showed large, stable effect sizes (Cohen's d = −3.36 to −3.86) at all lengths, and FE achieved lower mean runtimes despite higher success rates. These results demonstrate that elimination-based search with paradox retention gains a scaling advantage over standard Monte Carlo as combinatorial complexity increases. The experiment does not establish generality beyond the 3D HP lattice domain, does not extrapolate beyond L = 50, and does not claim FE superiority in all optimization regimes.
