


Strategic Forgetting with Paradox Retention Improves Stochastic Optimization in Lattice Protein Folding

Derek L. Angell
CONEXUS Global Arts Media, Winter Springs, Florida, USA Correspondence: derek@conexusglobal.ai




Abstract

Stochastic optimization methods for protein structure prediction must balance exploration and exploitation over extremely rugged energy landscapes. Classical Monte Carlo approaches often converge prematurely and sample conformational space inefficiently. Here I introduce a population-based metaheuristic that inverts the usual “accumulate the good” paradigm. The Forgetting Engine maintains a pool of candidate structures and iteratively (i) eliminates the worst-performing fraction, while (ii) retaining a compact archive of structurally “paradoxical” features from discarded candidates that are periodically reintroduced.

On three-dimensional hydrophobic–polar (HP) lattice folding benchmarks comprising 4,000 independent trials (2,000 per algorithm), the Forgetting Engine achieves a 25.8% success rate in reaching known optimal energies, versus 3.9% for a Monte Carlo control under matched computational budgets—a 561% relative improvement. Mean final energy improves by 2.09 units (−8.91 vs −6.82; p < 0.001, Cohen’s d = 1.53). Trial-level analysis shows that higher paradox-archive activity is positively associated with success.

These results demonstrate that strategic forgetting—the deliberate removal of information combined with targeted reintroduction of contradictory structure—can act as an effective optimization primitive for combinatorial search, suggesting applications beyond protein folding.




1. Introduction

Predicting protein structure from sequence remains a central challenge in computational biology. The number of possible conformations for even modest polypeptides grows exponentially with chain length, placing full exploration of the conformational landscape far beyond brute-force enumeration and situating protein folding among canonical NP-hard problems.

Stochastic sampling methods, particularly Monte Carlo (MC)–based strategies, have long provided practical tools for probing these landscapes. By accepting or rejecting proposed structural moves as a function of their energy differences, such methods can in principle escape local minima via thermal fluctuations. In practice, however, standard implementations often suffer from premature convergence, limited coverage of distant basins, and difficulty balancing local refinement against global exploration.

Population-based metaheuristics—genetic algorithms, particle swarm optimization, and related approaches—address some of these limitations by maintaining a diverse set of candidates that evolve jointly. These frameworks are typically accumulative: they identify promising structures, recombine or mutate them, and preferentially propagate improved lineages. The complementary operation—systematic removal of candidates—usually appears only indirectly (for example, via tournament selection) and is rarely treated as the central computational primitive.

Here I explore the opposite stance: what happens if we make elimination the main driver of search? The proposed method, termed the Forgetting Engine (FE), starts from a simple hypothesis:

Optimization may be accelerated not only by learning from good candidates, but by aggressively forgetting bad ones—while preserving a structured trace of what made them interesting.

The Forgetting Engine implements this idea via two coupled mechanisms:

1.  Strategic elimination – each iteration removes a fixed fraction of the lowest-fitness candidates.

2.  Paradox retention – before elimination, the algorithm extracts structurally diverse “paradoxical” features from the discarded set and stores them in a compact archive. These features are then used to generate new candidates in subsequent iterations.


Intuitively, the algorithm treats failure as informative: dead-end structures help delineate where not to search, while a minority of contradictory configurations may harbour motifs that become useful in different contexts.

To assess this framework in a controlled setting, I evaluate the Forgetting Engine on three-dimensional HP lattice protein folding, a standard benchmark that captures key combinatorial aspects of the folding problem while permitting extensive replication. Under matched computational budgets, the Forgetting Engine substantially outperforms a Monte Carlo control in both success rate and final energy. I further analyse the relation between paradox-archive activity and optimization outcomes, providing evidence that the retention mechanism contributes meaningfully to performance.

2. Algorithm

2.1 Overview

The Forgetting Engine maintains a population ( P_t ) of candidate structures at iteration ( t ), along with a separate paradox archive ( A_t ) that stores structural features extracted from eliminated candidates. Each iteration consists of four phases: evaluation, elimination, paradox archival, and regeneration.

Key hyperparameters used in the present study are:

●   Population size ( N = 100 )

●   Maximum iterations per trial = 500

●   Elimination rate ( \varepsilon = 0.20 ) (bottom 20% removed each iteration)

●   Paradox retention strength ( \rho = 0.15 ) (proportion of new candidates influenced by archive features)


2.2 Evaluation phase

For each candidate structure ( x \in P_t ), the algorithm computes an energy score ( E(x) ) using the HP lattice energy function (Section 4.1). Lower values correspond to more favourable folds.

2.3 Strategic elimination phase

Candidates are sorted by energy, and the worst-performing fraction (bottom ( \varepsilon N )) is removed.   This   aggressive   pruning   contrasts   with   softer  selection  mechanisms  (e.g., tournament selection) in which low-fitness candidates may persist through stochastic effects.

2.4 Paradox archival phase

Before elimination, structural descriptors are extracted from the soon-to-be-discarded candidates and added to a fixed-size archive ( A_t ) (up to 50 entries in these experiments). Descriptors emphasize diversity relative to the retained population—for example, backbone contact patterns or local motif frequencies that are under-represented among survivors. The goal is to capture “contradictory” features that deviate from the current search direction but may prove useful later.

2.5 Regeneration phase

Eliminated candidates are replaced to restore population size. New structures are generated by mutating retained candidates, with mutation operators biased by features sampled from the paradox archive. A fraction ( \rho ) of new candidates incorporate such archive-guided mutations; the remainder are generated via standard local moves. This coupling reintroduces structural diversity lost through elimination while ensuring that regenerated structures remain anchored to partially fit configurations.

2.6 Conceptual interpretation

The combination of strategic elimination and paradox retention explicitly manages the exploration–exploitation trade-off. Elimination heightens selection pressure and focuses computation on promising basins, while the archive injects controlled, diversity-preserving noise. By archiving features rather than whole candidates, the method decouples diversity maintenance from direct survival of low-fitness structures, yielding a compact and dynamically relevant memory.




3. Results

3.1 Benchmark and study design

The Forgetting Engine was evaluated on three-dimensional HP lattice protein folding, represented as self-avoiding walks on a cubic lattice with hydrophobic (H) and polar (P) residues. The energy function assigns −1 for each pair of non-sequential hydrophobic residues occupying adjacent lattice sites and 0 otherwise. Despite its simplicity, the HP model remains computationally demanding for sequences beyond ~50 residues and is widely used as a benchmark for optimization methods.

Benchmark sequences consisted of HP chains of 50–60 residues with known or computationally verified optimal energies. Each algorithm—Monte Carlo (control) and Forgetting Engine (experimental)—received identical computational budgets per trial: population size ( N = 100 ), 500 iterations, and equivalent numbers of energy evaluations. The study comprised 4,000 independent trials: 2,000 Monte Carlo and 2,000 Forgetting Engine. Random seeds were fixed and logged for reproducibility. Success was defined as reaching a final energy at or below the known optimum for the corresponding sequence.

3.2 Primary outcomes

Across all trials, the Forgetting Engine achieved substantially higher success rates than Monte Carlo (Table 1). Of 2,000 FE trials, 516 (25.8%) met the success criterion, compared to 78 of 2,000 Monte Carlo trials (3.9%), corresponding to a 561% relative improvement. Mean final energy improved from −6.82 (MC) to −8.91 (FE), and the best energy found improved from

−11.22 to −12.18. FE also exhibited lower variance in final energy (standard deviation 1.28 vs 1.45).

A two-sample t-test with Welch’s correction confirmed that differences in mean energy were highly significant (t = −48.3, p < 0.001), with a large effect size (Cohen’s d = −1.53).

3.3 Paradox retention and trial success

To assess the role of paradox retention, the Forgetting Engine recorded the number of times, per trial, that regenerated candidates incorporated features sampled from the paradox archive (“paradox activity”). Successful trials showed higher average paradox activity (15.2 events per trial; 95% CI 14.6–15.8) than unsuccessful trials (13.8; 95% CI 13.5–14.1). This difference was statistically significant (p < 0.01), indicating that archive-mediated diversity contributes meaningfully to optimization performance.

3.4 Preliminary two-dimensional experiments

Before the 3D benchmark campaign, a separate experiment evaluated FE on a 20-residue 2D HP lattice sequence with known ground state energy −8. Over 1,000 trials per algorithm, FE achieved a 45% success rate versus 25% for Monte Carlo, converged approximately 2.15× faster on successful trials (367 vs 789 steps), and reached lower mean final energies (−3.67 vs −2.34). All differences were statistically significant (p < 0.001).

These preliminary results suggested that the relative advantage of FE increases with problem complexity, motivating the larger-scale 3D study reported here.




4. Discussion

The present results show that an elimination-centric metaheuristic with paradox retention can substantially improve stochastic optimization performance on protein-folding benchmarks

relative to a Monte Carlo baseline under matched computational budgets. The 561% improvement in success rate and large effect size in mean energy indicate that the gains are not merely incremental.

4.1 Why strategic forgetting helps

Several mechanisms likely underlie the effectiveness of strategic forgetting:

1.  Focused resource allocation. Aggressive elimination of low-fitness candidates concentrates computation on more promising regions of conformational space, reducing wasted evaluations.

2.  Explicit selection pressure. By making elimination the primary operator, FE enforces a  strong  and  transparent  selection  criterion,  unlike  softer  mechanisms  in  which low-fitness candidates persist stochastically.

3.  Controlled diversity through paradox retention. Pure elimination would risk rapid but premature convergence. The paradox archive provides a structured way to reintroduce diversity, guided by features that are rare but potentially valuable. The positive correlation between paradox activity and success supports this interpretation.


These mechanisms resonate with broader themes in computational intelligence, where memory-efficient methods that selectively discard information can outperform naïvely
accumulative counterparts in streaming and online learning settings. The present work suggests that similar principles apply to combinatorial optimization.

4.2 Limitations

Several limitations temper the scope of these findings:

●   Simplified physics. The HP lattice model is a coarse abstraction of real protein energetics. Extension to off-lattice models, explicit solvent, or all-atom force fields will be necessary to assess biological utility.



●   Baseline selection. Monte Carlo was chosen as the baseline because it remains the most widely used stochastic sampling method in protein‑folding benchmarks and provides a clear reference point for elimination‑centric strategies. While comparisons here are restricted to Monte Carlo, future work should benchmark the Forgetting Engine against state‑of‑the‑art metaheuristics such as genetic algorithms, parallel tempering, and hybrid approaches to establish relative performance across the broader optimization landscape.

●   Hyperparameter tuning. Paradox retention parameters were tuned on the study sequences and may not be optimal for unseen sequences or different problem classes. Independent validation and automated tuning procedures are needed.

●   Wall-clock performance. This study focused on success rates and energy metrics under matched evaluation budgets. Systematic profiling of wall-clock runtimes on varied hardware would clarify practical deployment costs.


4.3 Outlook and broader applications

Despite these limitations, the Forgetting Engine illustrates that structured forgetting can serve as a productive computational primitive. Elimination is typically treated as a side effect of selection; here it becomes the main lever that shapes the search process. This conceptual shift opens several directions:

●   Application to other NP-hard problems such as travelling-salesperson variants, graph colouring, and vehicle routing, where preliminary internal experiments already suggest similar scaling behaviour.

●   Integration with learning-based models, where FE could operate as a
meta-optimizer over architectures or hyperparameters, using paradox retention to maintain diverse inductive biases.

●   Theoretical analysis of convergence and diversity maintenance under different elimination and retention schedules.


More broadly, treating “what to forget” as a first-class design question may yield new families of algorithms that are robust, data-efficient, and well-suited to emerging high-dimensional optimization tasks.
Beyond lattice folding, this approach could accelerate drug discovery by revealing metastable states overlooked by additive models, and more broadly improve optimization in biological and medical domains where conventional strategies falter.






5. Methods

5.1 Hydrophobic–polar lattice model

Proteins were represented using the three-dimensional hydrophobic–polar lattice model. Sequences of length 50–60 residues were encoded as strings over {H, P}. Each structure is a self-avoiding walk on a cubic lattice, with residues occupying lattice vertices and bonds represented by unit-length edges. The energy function

[
E(x) = - \sum_{(i,j)} \mathbb{I}[x_i = H, x_j = H, \text{non-sequential neighbours}] ]

assigns −1 for each pair of non-sequential hydrophobic residues adjacent on the lattice, and 0 otherwise.

5.2 Forgetting Engine implementation

The Forgetting Engine was implemented in Python 3.8 with NumPy for numerical operations. Key parameters:

●   Population size = 100

●   Maximum iterations per trial = 500

●   Elimination rate ( \varepsilon = 0.20 )

●   Paradox archive capacity = 50 descriptors

●   Paradox-influenced regeneration fraction ( \rho = 0.15 )


Each iteration comprised:

1.  Evaluation: compute energy for all candidates.

2.  Elimination: sort by energy, remove bottom 20%.

3.  Paradox archival: update archive with structurally diverse descriptors drawn from eliminated candidates.

4.  Regeneration: generate replacement candidates from retained ones via mutation operators, with a subset guided by archive descriptors.


Mutation operators included local pivot moves and segment rotations, constrained to maintain self-avoidance.

5.3 Monte Carlo implementation

The Monte Carlo control received identical budgets: population size 100, 500 iterations, same set of legal moves, and the same energy function. At each step, new candidate structures were generated via random local moves; proposals were accepted or rejected according to the Metropolis criterion at fixed temperature. The best-performing candidates populated the next generation; no elimination, paradox archival, or structured regeneration mechanisms were employed.

5.4 Statistical analysis

Primary endpoints were success rate and mean final energy per trial. Success rates were compared using Fisher’s exact test; energies were compared using two-sample t-tests with Welch’s correction for unequal variances. Effect sizes were summarised as Cohen’s d, and 95% confidence intervals were obtained via bootstrap resampling (10,000 iterations). All analyses were conducted in Python using SciPy 1.9.

5.5 Reproducibility

Random seeds for the Monte Carlo and Forgetting Engine trials were initialised separately (seed_MC = 42, seed_FE = 43); per-trial seeds were generated deterministically from these bases to enable exact replication. Full parameter settings, seeds, and analysis scripts are provided in the accompanying code repository and supplementary data package.

Figure 4 – SFE Optimization Workflow

Figure 4 presents a side-by-side workflow comparison between a standard Monte Carlo pipeline and the Strategic Forgetting Engine (SFE).
On the left, the baseline Monte Carlo chain is shown as a sequence of grey boxes: Initialization → Candidate Generation → Energy Evaluation → Acceptance / Rejection → Next Step. This emphasizes that the classical method samples one configuration at a time and decides locally whether to keep or discard it, with no long-term memory of alternatives.

On the right, the SFE pipeline is rendered in teal and introduces two additional modules that constitute the core of the method’s novelty. After Initialization, states enter a Candidate Pool, where a batch of configurations is evaluated in parallel by the Fitness Evaluation step. The Strategic Forgetting module then prunes the bottom fraction of candidates (e.g., lowest 30 % by fitness), explicitly freeing capacity in the search space. Crucially, before these states are discarded, atypical high-complexity or high-potential candidates are diverted into the Paradox

Retention module and stored in a Paradox Registry. The remaining high-fitness states are recombined into a New Generation, closing the loop.

By visually contrasting the two vertical pipelines and highlighting the SFE-only modules, the figure makes clear that SFE is not just “faster Monte Carlo,” but an architectural shift: it separates forgetting from preservation, turning elimination and anomaly-tracking into first-class operations within the optimization process.

































Figure 5 – SFE Speedup for Larger Problem Sizes

Figure 5 quantifies how the computational advantage of SFE scales with problem size. The x-axis denotes sequence length (residues) in the HP lattice model, while the y-axis shows speedup relative to a standard Monte Carlo baseline (×). A teal curve with circular markers at selected lengths (e.g., 20, 40, 60, 80, 100 residues) begins near 1× for the smallest instances and rises monotonically toward higher speedup factors at larger sizes. A horizontal grey dashed line at 1× (“No Speedup”) provides an intuitive reference.

Conceptually, the upward trend illustrates that as the combinatorial space explodes with sequence length, the benefit of strategically forgetting low-value regions grows. For short sequences, Monte Carlo can still wander into the global basin within reasonable time, so SFE’s advantage is modest. For longer sequences, however, naive random walks spend most of their time revisiting unpromising configurations, while SFE continuously contracts its search horizon and reuses information in the Paradox Registry. The result is an effective speedup that amplifies with scale, underscoring that SFE is particularly well-suited for large, rugged optimization landscapes.
